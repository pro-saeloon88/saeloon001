Affiliations

Saeloon had links to two distinct online groups:

  • SIMP / WHYTBS — A separate group chat, known as WHYTBS ("We Help You To Be a Simp"). Participant accounts describe it as problematic. Mohamed Khaled (Kahk) and Mohamed Ayman (JackDaPrince) were former members, with Kahk being one of the founders. This is a former, independent association, separate from Saeloon's core activities.
  • صالون فوزي الثقافي (Salon Fawzi Cultural) — An informal cultural circle where Kahk was sometimes a leader and always a respected member. Ahmed FwZzy led the group and had many conversations with Kahk. Salon Fawzi Cultural was considered a "twin country" to Saeloon, but a migration plan between the two groups failed due to member conflicts; a poll resulted in a majority voting against the plan.

Background

Saeloon was founded in 2020 by Mohamed Khaled and a small circle of friends, including Ziad al 7mir. It started as a casual space for sharing memes, informal conversation, and discussing everyday life. The chat had no fixed name at first, and titles were frequently changed for humorous effect.

Name & Identity

In early 2021, Mohamed Khaled proposed establishing a stable group identity. The name "Saeloon" was adopted to foster continuity and a sense of belonging among members.

Symbolism & Flag

Khaled designed a black-and-white skull flag inspired by the Jolly Roger using image-editing software. The flag served as an internal symbol rather than a political or national emblem.

Organization & Membership

Saeloon had no formal governance, written rules, or territory. Leadership was informal and based on participation and influence. Membership changed over time, with a core group of long-standing participants regarded as the founders.

Ahmalid Incident

Ahmalid was the youngest participant and left after a conflict on external platforms. Participant accounts:

"Since when you are my friend?" — Ahmalid
"Ok" — Kahk
"Your apology is useless to me." — Ahmalid

Kahk blocked Ahmalid from all Saeloon-related apps. Participants note Ahmalid followed atheist Egyptian figures online, interpreted as influence from external communities.

Security Incident Involving Mazen

From 2020–2022, a prolonged dispute occurred between Kahk and Mazen Mohamed (Port Said). Key points:

  • In 2020 Kahk was in his "memer prime" creating memes on a new Facebook profile.
  • Kahk became friends with Mazen Mohamed, a prankster with technical knowledge. Together they created "INFO • SCP".
  • The group grew to ~300 members in one month, but Mazen engaged in pranks and social engineering.
  • After ~1 year, the group's administration pages were banned on Facebook due to false reports, allegedly by Mazen. Kahk blocked him.
  • Mazen allegedly burned Kahk's phone motherboard; Kahk only realized months later when accessing his account from another device.
  • Kahk then created a new Facebook account "Zuck Bug" with enhanced security. Mazen eventually lost interest, allowing Kahk to continue Saeloon.

Influences & Internal Change

Internet culture and alternative media influenced the group, including the work of Jack Stauber. Personal changes, such as Saged Khaled's religious reorientation, affected group dynamics. Some communications moved to private chats like "Sanitarium".

Dissolution

On 5 November 2024, Kahk announced the closure of Saeloon public chats. Final message:

Arabic: "@الكل هذا سيكون كافي ايها الرفاق ويا شعب السايلون العظيم يجب ان نرحل الان ولكن السايلون سيبقى في قلوبنا للابد، أستودعكم الله الذي لا تضيع ودائعَه"
English: "@Everyone, this will be enough, comrades, and to the great people of Saeloon. We must leave now, but Saeloon will remain in our hearts forever. I entrust you to God, whose trust is never lost."

Some members continued limited private interactions in "Sanitarium".

Analysis

Saeloon is an example of symbolic micronationalism online: fictional statehood and shared symbols strengthened in-group cohesion and identity.

Legacy

Though public activity ended, Saeloon continues in memory, archives, and copied materials. It is primarily an archival artifact rather than a living online community.
